\section*{Abstract}
\addcontentsline{toc}{section}{Abstract}

This project investigates the design and simulation of terahertz coplanar
waveguide (CPW) resonators using Ansys Lumerical FDTD. The aim is to
understand how cavity length and capacitive coupling geometry control the
resonance frequency, transmission response and loaded quality factor~($Q_L$).
A half-wavelength CPW cavity with a centre conductor width of
$s = \SI{10.05}{\micro\metre}$ and a slot gap of $w = \SI{6.6}{\micro\metre}$
was designed for a fundamental resonance near \SI{0.8}{\tera\hertz}.

A cavity length sweep confirmed the inverse frequency--length relationship
and yielded a corrected effective refractive index $n_{\mathrm{eff}} = 2.68$
and an end correction $\Delta L \approx \SI{12.4}{\micro\metre}$, consistent
with capacitive loading at the coupling gaps. A capacitive gap sweep with
gaps from \SI{0.8}{\micro\metre} to \SI{5}{\micro\metre} produced $Q_L$
values of 6.3 to 13.6, confirming that larger gaps give weaker coupling and
higher quality factors. An interdigital capacitor (IDC) coupling structure
with 5+5 fingers was subsequently simulated; the IDC loading shifted the
resonance to approximately \SI{0.45}{\tera\hertz} and produced a very broad
transmission response ($Q_L \approx 1.2$), indicating severe overcoupling.
The project demonstrates that CPW resonator behaviour can be engineered
through cavity geometry and coupling design, and identifies finger count
reduction and tip-gap enlargement as the primary routes to higher~$Q_L$.

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

\subsection{Background}

Coplanar waveguides (CPWs) are widely used in microwave, millimetre-wave
and terahertz circuits because they provide compact planar transmission
lines that can be fabricated using standard photolithographic
processes~\cite{Wen1969}. A CPW consists of a centre signal conductor
flanked by two lateral ground planes, separated by narrow slots. The
electromagnetic field is concentrated in the gap region, making CPWs
well suited for compact resonators and integrated sensing devices.

The terahertz frequency range (approximately \SIrange{0.1}{10}{\tera\hertz})
is of growing importance for high-speed wireless communication, imaging,
molecular spectroscopy and quantum information
systems~\cite{Siegel2002}. However, the wavelength at terahertz
frequencies is on the order of hundreds of micrometres, so resonators
must be designed at the micrometre scale. Substrate permittivity, metal
conductivity and coupling geometry each play a significant role in the
frequency response and quality factor of the device.

\subsection{Motivation}

This project is motivated by the need to understand how CPW resonator
geometry can be used to engineer resonance frequency and quality factor
at terahertz frequencies. Two coupling strategies are investigated:
simple capacitive gap coupling and interdigital capacitor (IDC) coupling.
The IDC structure offers greater design flexibility, but introduces a
more complex capacitive loading that must be carefully characterised.

\subsection{Aim}

The aim is to design, simulate and analyse terahertz CPW resonators
using full-wave FDTD simulation, with focus on how coupling geometry
controls resonance frequency, bandwidth and loaded quality factor.

\subsection{Objectives}

\begin{enumerate}[label=\textbf{O\arabic*:}]
    \item Review relevant literature on CPW resonator theory, terahertz
          devices and FDTD simulation.
    \item Design a half-wavelength CPW resonator targeting resonance near
          \SI{0.8}{\tera\hertz}.
    \item Build and simulate the resonator geometry in Ansys Lumerical FDTD.
    \item Characterise the relationship between cavity length and resonance
          frequency and extract the effective refractive index and end
          correction.
    \item Investigate simple capacitive gap coupling and its effect on
          $Q_L$ over a range of gap widths.
    \item Design and simulate an IDC-coupled resonator and characterise the
          effect of the finger geometry on coupling strength.
    \item Extract resonance frequency, FWHM bandwidth and $Q_L$ by
          Lorentzian fitting in Python.
    \item Compare simulated results with transmission-line theory and
          derive design guidelines for higher-$Q_L$ CPW resonators.
\end{enumerate}

\Cref{sec:litreview} reviews the relevant literature.

\newpage
\section{Literature Review}
\label{sec:litreview}

\subsection{Coplanar Waveguide Transmission Lines}

The coplanar waveguide was introduced by Wen~\cite{Wen1969}, who derived
the quasi-static field distribution using conformal mapping. The CPW
supports a quasi-TEM mode whose field is concentrated in the slot region
between the signal conductor and the ground planes, making the structure
attractive for planar circuit integration. Simons~\cite{Simons2001}
provides a comprehensive treatment of CPW transmission lines, covering
effective permittivity, characteristic impedance, discontinuities and
transitions. The effective permittivity is accurately described by
elliptic integral expressions derived from conformal mapping and depends
on the ratio of conductor width to slot width as well as the substrate
thickness and permittivity~\cite{Ghione1987}.

\subsection{Microwave and Terahertz Resonators}

CPW resonators are widely used as building blocks for filters, oscillators
and sensing systems at microwave and millimetre-wave
frequencies~\cite{Pozar2011}. The loaded quality factor depends on both
internal dissipation and external coupling strength. For superconducting
CPW resonators used in circuit quantum electrodynamics,
G\"{o}ppl~\textit{et al.}~\cite{Goppl2008} demonstrated $Q_L$ values
exceeding $10^6$ at cryogenic temperatures. At terahertz frequencies and
room temperature, conductor losses and radiation limit the achievable
$Q_L$ to much lower values~\cite{Siegel2002}. This project focuses on
the regime where the coupling geometry, rather than material loss,
dominates the loaded quality factor.

\subsection{Capacitive Coupling Structures}

Coupling between a feedline and a resonator is commonly achieved using a
simple capacitive gap or an interdigital capacitor (IDC). Pozar~\cite{Pozar2011}
shows that the coupling quality factor is inversely proportional to the
square of the coupling capacitance: a larger capacitance gives lower
$Q_c$ and lower $Q_L$. IDC structures, in which interleaved metal fingers
increase the effective electrode area, are used where a large coupling
capacitance is required in a compact footprint~\cite{Simons2001}. The
coupling capacitance of an IDC scales approximately linearly with the
finger count and the finger overlap length. A key design challenge with
IDC coupling is that the finger structure also loads the resonator,
shifting the resonance frequency downward.

\subsection{FDTD Simulation}

The finite-difference time-domain (FDTD) method~\cite{Taflove2005} is
widely used for full-wave electromagnetic simulation of planar resonators.
Maxwell's equations are solved on a Cartesian grid in the time domain, and
frequency-domain $S$-parameters are obtained by Fourier transformation.
Perfectly matched layer (PML) absorbing boundaries are used to terminate
the simulation domain without artificial reflections. For CPW structures
containing sub-micrometre coupling gaps, local mesh refinement is required
to resolve the coupling region accurately.

\subsection{Quality Factor Extraction}

Khalil~\textit{et al.}~\cite{Khalil2012} describe robust methods for
extracting the loaded quality factor from two-port $S$-parameter data.
For a symmetric two-port transmission resonator, $Q_L = f_0/\Delta f$,
where $\Delta f$ is the FWHM of the transmission peak, is directly
applicable. More advanced circle-fitting methods can separate $Q_L$ into
internal and coupling contributions, but require accurate measurement of
the on-resonance transmission level and coupling asymmetry.

% ============================================================
\newpage
\section{Theoretical Background}
\label{sec:theory}

\subsection{CPW Geometry}

A CPW consists of three co-planar metal conductors on a dielectric
substrate: a centre signal conductor of width~$s$ and two semi-infinite
ground planes separated from the signal conductor by slots of width~$w$.
The geometry is parameterised by the ratio $k = s/(s + 2w)$, which
determines the effective permittivity and the characteristic impedance.
The electromagnetic field is predominantly concentrated in the two slots,
providing strong localised field enhancement that is useful for coupling
and sensing.

\subsection{CPW Effective Permittivity}

For a CPW on a substrate of relative permittivity $\epsilon_r$ and
thickness $h$, the quasi-static effective permittivity is derived using
conformal mapping as~\cite{Ghione1987,Simons2001}:
\begin{equation}
    \epsilon_{\mathrm{eff}} = 1 +
    \frac{\epsilon_r - 1}{2}\,
    \frac{K(k_1)}{K(k_1')}\,
    \frac{K(k')}{K(k)},
    \label{eq:eps_eff_full}
\end{equation}
where $K(\cdot)$ is the complete elliptic integral of the first kind,
$k = s/(s+2w)$, $k' = \sqrt{1-k^2}$, and $k_1$, $k_1'$ are modified
arguments that account for the finite substrate thickness. For substrates
much thicker than the conductor dimensions ($h \gg s + 2w$),
\cref{eq:eps_eff_full} reduces to the commonly used approximation:
\begin{equation}
    \epsilon_{\mathrm{eff}} \approx \frac{\epsilon_r + 1}{2}.
    \label{eq:eps_eff_simple}
\end{equation}
The effective refractive index of the CPW quasi-TEM mode is:
\begin{equation}
    n_{\mathrm{eff}} = \sqrt{\epsilon_{\mathrm{eff}}}.
    \label{eq:neff}
\end{equation}
For a high-resistivity silicon substrate ($\epsilon_r \approx 11.7$),
\cref{eq:eps_eff_simple,eq:neff} give $n_{\mathrm{eff}} \approx 2.52$.
The value $n_{\mathrm{eff}} = 2.65$ used for initial cavity design
corresponds to a slightly higher effective permittivity, consistent with
a substrate having $\epsilon_r \approx 13$ (e.g.\ GaAs or similar
compound semiconductor) or with a correction for the specific $s/w$
ratio and finite substrate thickness.

\subsection{Half-Wavelength Resonator}

A half-wavelength resonator requires the electric field to vanish
(short-circuit boundary) at both ends of the cavity. The fundamental
resonance frequency is:
\begin{equation}
    f_0 = \frac{c}{2\,n_{\mathrm{eff}}\,L_{\mathrm{cav}}}.
    \label{eq:halfwave}
\end{equation}
Rearranging \cref{eq:halfwave} gives the required cavity length:
\begin{equation}
    L_{\mathrm{cav}} = \frac{c}{2\,n_{\mathrm{eff}}\,f_0}.
    \label{eq:Lcav}
\end{equation}
For $f_0 = \SI{0.8}{\tera\hertz}$ and $n_{\mathrm{eff}} = 2.65$,
\cref{eq:Lcav} gives $L_{\mathrm{cav}} \approx \SI{70.75}{\micro\metre}$.

\subsection{End Correction}

In practice, the physical cavity length does not fully describe the
resonator. Fringing fields at the coupling gaps and capacitive loading
at the cavity ends increase the effective electrical length. This is
modelled by an end correction~$\Delta L$:
\begin{equation}
    f_0 = \frac{c}{2\,n_{\mathrm{eff}}\,(L_{\mathrm{cav}} + \Delta L)}.
    \label{eq:end_correction}
\end{equation}
Fitting \cref{eq:end_correction} to simulated resonance frequencies at
different cavity lengths allows both $n_{\mathrm{eff}}$ and $\Delta L$
to be extracted simultaneously.

\subsection{Loaded Quality Factor}

The loaded quality factor is:
\begin{equation}
    Q_L = \frac{f_0}{\Delta f},
    \label{eq:QL}
\end{equation}
where $\Delta f$ is the FWHM bandwidth of the resonance peak. The loaded
quality factor is related to the internal and coupling contributions by:
\begin{equation}
    \frac{1}{Q_L} = \frac{1}{Q_i} + \frac{1}{Q_c}.
    \label{eq:Q_relation}
\end{equation}
To maximise $Q_L$, both the internal loss ($1/Q_i$) and the coupling
strength ($1/Q_c$) must be minimised.

\subsection{S-Parameters and Resonance Response}

The two-port $S$-parameters describe the scattering of signals between
ports. The transmission coefficient $S_{21}$ reaches a local maximum at
the resonance frequency, where stored energy is released to both ports.
The reflection coefficient $S_{11}$ simultaneously reaches a minimum.
For a symmetric lossless resonator with equal port coupling,
$|S_{21}| = 1$ and $|S_{11}| = 0$ at resonance.

\subsection{Lorentzian Fitting}

The squared magnitude of the transmission coefficient near resonance
follows a Lorentzian lineshape:
\begin{equation}
    |S_{21}|^2 = b_0 + A\,
    \frac{(\Delta f/2)^2}{(f - f_0)^2 + (\Delta f/2)^2},
    \label{eq:lorentzian}
\end{equation}
where $b_0$ is a background offset, $A$ is the peak amplitude and
$\Delta f$ is the FWHM. The fitted $f_0$ and $\Delta f$ are substituted
into \cref{eq:QL} to obtain $Q_L$.

\newpage
\section{Simulation Methodology}
\label{sec:methodology}

\subsection{Software Platform}

All electromagnetic simulations were performed in Ansys Lumerical
FDTD~\cite{Lumerical2024}. The simulation geometry, mesh and analysis
objects were defined using the Lumerical Scripting Language (LSF),
enabling parameterised and reproducible setups. The maximum FDTD simulation
time was set to \SI{100}{\pico\second} (\num{100000}~fs) for all simulated
geometries to ensure sufficient time for the high-Q resonant fields to
decay, allowing the early auto-shutoff mechanism to trigger only when the
fields reached the required threshold.

\subsection{Simulation Domain and Boundary Conditions}

The three-dimensional simulation domain was terminated on all six faces
using perfectly matched layer (PML) absorbing boundary conditions, which
suppress reflections from the domain edges without introducing artificial
boundary effects~\cite{Taflove2005}. The CPW mode was excited using
input and output ports placed at both ends of the feedlines. Each port
used a mode source based on the fundamental CPW quasi-TEM mode profile to
ensure that only the desired mode was launched and measured.

\subsection{Material Model}

Metal conductors (signal line and ground planes) were assigned the PEC
model. This idealised model is appropriate for an initial analysis
because it isolates geometric and coupling effects from
frequency-dependent conductor losses, enabling a clean study of how
geometry alone controls the resonance frequency and coupling strength.

\subsection{Mesh Strategy}

A non-uniform mesh was used throughout the simulation domain. Global
mesh accuracy was set to ensure adequate resolution of the CPW feedlines
and resonator cavity. Local mesh override regions were applied around
each coupling structure to resolve sub-micrometre features:

\begin{table}[htbp]
    \centering
    \caption{Mesh override regions and target cell sizes used in the FDTD simulation.}
    \label{tab:mesh}
    \begin{tabular}{lcc}
        \toprule
        Region & $\Delta x$, $\Delta y$ & $\Delta z$ \\
        \midrule
        IDC finger and gap region    & \SIrange{0.05}{0.10}{\micro\metre}
                                     & \SIrange{0.2}{0.5}{\micro\metre} \\
        Capacitive gap coupling      & \SIrange{0.10}{0.20}{\micro\metre}
                                     & \SIrange{0.2}{0.5}{\micro\metre} \\
        General CPW feedline region  & \SIrange{0.5}{1.0}{\micro\metre}
                                     & \SIrange{0.5}{1.0}{\micro\metre} \\
        \bottomrule
    \end{tabular}
\end{table}

Fine mesh was applied only in the coupling regions to limit the total
cell count and simulation runtime.

\subsection{Data Processing}

The simulation exported frequency-domain transmission data as $|S_{21}|^2$.
Conversion to decibels used:
\begin{equation}
    S_{21,\mathrm{dB}} = 10\log_{10}(|S_{21}|^2).
    \label{eq:dB}
\end{equation}
For reference-normalised spectra, the resonator transmission was divided
by the transmission through a straight CPW reference line of identical
length:
\begin{equation}
    S_{21,\mathrm{norm,dB}} =
    10\log_{10}\!\left(\frac{|S_{21}|^2}{|S_{21,\mathrm{ref}}|^2}\right).
    \label{eq:norm}
\end{equation}
Post-processing used Python~3 with NumPy, SciPy and Matplotlib.
Lorentzian fitting was performed using \texttt{scipy.optimize.curve\_fit}
with the model of \cref{eq:lorentzian}.

\subsection{Gap Capacitor-Coupled Resonator}

The first coupling design used simple capacitive gaps. The CPW centre
conductor was divided into three sections: the input feedline, the
resonator cavity and the output feedline. Capacitive gaps of width $C_g$
were placed at $x = \pm L_{\mathrm{cav}}/2$ to electrically isolate the
cavity from the feedlines. Power is exchanged through the fringing
electric field across the gap.

The initial cavity length was $L_{\mathrm{cav}} = \SI{70.75}{\micro\metre}$
from \cref{eq:Lcav}. Gap widths of \SI{5}{\micro\metre},
\SI{3}{\micro\metre}, \SI{1}{\micro\metre} and \SI{0.8}{\micro\metre}
were simulated, covering a range from weak to strong coupling. Smaller
gaps increase the coupling capacitance, which lowers $Q_c$ and hence
$Q_L$ via \cref{eq:Q_relation}.

\subsection{Interdigital Capacitor-Coupled Resonator}

The second design replaced each capacitive gap with an IDC. Alternating
fingers connect to the feedline or to the cavity, forming a large
interdigitated coupling capacitor within the width of the signal
conductor. The structure was implemented as a single merged polygon in
Lumerical to avoid artificial metal--metal interfaces. The IDC parameters
are given in \cref{tab:idc_params}.

\subsection{Trench-Coupled Resonator}
\label{sec:trench_design}

To investigate whether a substrate-integrated trench structure could
improve electromagnetic confinement and reduce radiation losses, a third
resonator variant was designed in which the ground planes extend into
the substrate via air-filled trenches etched on either side of the CPW
slots. This geometry is motivated by the need to suppress parasitic
substrate modes at terahertz frequencies, which couple into the bulk
dielectric and degrade $Q_i$~\cite{Pozar2011}.

The trench structure consists of the following elements, all built
parameterically in Lumerical using a dedicated script
(\texttt{lumerical\_scripts/trench\_geometry.lsf}):

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Signal line} --- The CPW signal conductor is divided
          into three coplanar 2D rectangles (left feedline, cavity, and
          right feedline) separated by the coupling gap of length
          $C_g$ on each side, placed at $z = 0$ (the top metal surface).

    \item \textbf{Surface ground planes} --- Four surface rectangles
          define the ground metal at $z = 0$: an outer pair extending
          to $\pm\SI{1000}{\mu m}$ in $y$, and an inner pair that
          occupy the slot region between the signal edge $(s/2 + w)$
          and the trench inner wall $(s/2 + w + y_{\mathrm{height}})$.

    \item \textbf{Trench sidewalls} --- Vertical metal walls lining each
          trench are modelled as $y$-normal 2D rectangles at the four
          trench wall positions
          $y = \pm(s/2 + w + y_{\mathrm{height}})$ and
          $y = \pm(s/2 + w + y_{\mathrm{height}} + L_{\mathrm{buried}})$,
          spanning in depth from $z = -h_{\mathrm{buried}}$ to
          $z = 0$.

    \item \textbf{Trench floor panels} --- Horizontal metal rectangles
          at the bottom of each trench ($z = -h_{\mathrm{buried}}$)
          close the conductor channel, forming a continuous shielding
          enclosure.

    \item \textbf{Air volumes} --- Two 3D rectangular volumes
          (\texttt{addrect}) fill the trench interior with air
          ($\varepsilon_r = 1$), replacing the substrate dielectric
          within the trench and reducing the effective permittivity
          seen by the guided mode.
\end{enumerate}

The key geometric parameters of the trench design are defined in
\cref{tab:trench_params}.

\begin{table}[htbp]
    \centering
    \caption{Trench geometry parameters used in \texttt{trench\_geometry.lsf}, as set in the Lumerical user properties panel.}
    \label{tab:trench_params}
    \begin{tabular}{llrr}
        \toprule
        Parameter & Symbol & Value & Unit \\
        \midrule
        Signal conductor width    & $s$                      & 10  & \si{\micro\metre} \\
        Slot width                & $w$                      & 7.5 & \si{\micro\metre} \\
        Coupling gap length       & $C_g$                    & 3   & \si{\micro\metre} \\
        Cavity length             & $L_{\mathrm{cav}}$       & 80  & \si{\micro\metre} \\
        Ground offset above slot  & $y_{\mathrm{height}}$    & 5   & \si{\micro\metre} \\
        Trench width in $y$       & $L_{\mathrm{buried}}$    & 5   & \si{\micro\metre} \\
        Trench depth in $z$       & $h_{\mathrm{buried}}$    & 50  & \si{\micro\metre} \\
        Conductor material        & \texttt{material}        & \multicolumn{2}{l}{PEC (Perfect Electrical Conductor)} \\
        Trench fill material      & \texttt{air}             & \multicolumn{2}{l}{etch} \\
        \bottomrule
    \end{tabular}
\end{table}

The core of the Lumerical script is reproduced below. The script
begins with \texttt{deleteall} to clear any existing geometry, then
builds each structural element in sequence:

\begin{lstlisting}[language=Lumerical, style=lumerical,
    caption={Key excerpt from \texttt{trench\_geometry.lsf}: signal
    line, ground planes, and trench sidewall construction.},
    label={lst:trench}]
# Signal line (left feed)
add2drect;
set("name","signal");
set("y min",-s/2);  set("y max",s/2);
set("x min",-2000e-6);  set("x max",-cav_L/2-C_g);
set("z",0e-6);  set("material",material);

# Upper trench inner sidewall (y-normal)
add2drect;
set("name","trench_upper_bot_inner");
set("surface normal",2);
set("y",(s/2+w+y_height));
set("x min",-2000e-6);  set("x max",2000e-6);
set("z",-h_burried/2);  set("z span",h_burried);
set("material",material);

# Upper air trench volume
addrect;
set("name","air1");
set("y min",(s/2+w+y_height));
set("y max",(s/2+w+y_height+L_burried));
set("z min",-h_burried);  set("z max",0e-6);
set("material",air);
\end{lstlisting}

The physical design intent is that the metal-lined trench channels guide
the field laterally and downward, preventing leakage into the substrate
bulk. The air infill lowers the effective permittivity adjacent to the
slot, which is expected to increase the phase velocity of the guided
mode and shift $f_0$ upward relative to a conventional substrate-backed
CPW of the same drawn length.

\subsection{Effective Index and End Correction}

The length sweep data were fitted to the end-correction model of
\cref{eq:end_correction} using a simultaneous least-squares fit for
$n_{\mathrm{eff}}$ and $\Delta L$. The fitted parameters are:
\begin{align}
    n_{\mathrm{eff}} &\approx 2.68,              \label{eq:fit_neff} \\
    \Delta L         &\approx \SI{12.4}{\micro\metre}. \label{eq:fit_dL}
\end{align}
The end correction of \SI{12.4}{\micro\metre} indicates that the
resonator responds as though the cavity is approximately
\SI{12}{\micro\metre} longer per end than the drawn metal length, due to
fringing fields and phase accumulation in the capacitive gap region.
\Cref{fig:end_correction_fit} shows the fit alongside the simpler
half-wavelength model.

\end{figure}

\subsection{IDC-Coupled Resonator}

The IDC-coupled resonator was simulated to evaluate the impact of the interdigital finger coupling on the resonator performance. Initially, the baseline configuration with $N = 5$ fingers per side ($N=5+5$ total) and a middle cavity length $L_{\mathrm{cav}} = \SI{40}{\micro\metre}$ was simulated over \SIrange{0}{1.5}{\tera\hertz}. The resulting $|S_{21}|$ transmission spectrum is shown in \cref{fig:idc_5x5_s21}.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.82\textwidth]{idc_5x5_s21.png}

    with $N = 5+5$ fingers and $L_{\mathrm{cav}} = \SI{40}{\micro\metre}$.
    The dominant peak near \SI{0.45}{\tera\hertz} indicates a highly
    overcoupled resonator with $Q_L \approx 1.2$.}
    \label{fig:idc_5x5_s21}
\end{figure}

The dominant peak is centred near \SI{0.45}{\tera\hertz}, well below the target frequency of \SI{0.8}{\tera\hertz}. The extracted FWHM is approximately \SI{387}{\giga\hertz}, yielding an extremely low quality factor $Q_L \approx 1.2$, indicating that the 10-finger geometry leads to excessive capacitive coupling.

To address this overcoupling, a parametric sweep of the finger count $N$ was conducted. The number of fingers per side was varied as $N = 3, 5$, and $7$, while keeping the rest of the resonator geometry and the coupling gap sizes constant. The simulated transmission squared ($|S_{21}|^2$) spectra for these three configurations are plotted in \cref{fig:idc_sweep_s21}, and the extracted resonance parameters are summarised in \cref{tab:idc_sweep_params}.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=1.0\textwidth]{idc_sweep_s21.png}
    \caption{Simulated $|S_{21}|^2$ transmission spectra of the IDC-coupled resonator for three finger counts ($N = 3, 5$, and $7$ fingers per side). All three configurations exhibit broad, low-contrast resonances characteristic of an overcoupled system ($Q_L < 1$).}
    \label{fig:idc_sweep_s21}
\end{figure}

\begin{table}[htbp]
    \centering
    \caption{Extracted resonance frequency, FWHM bandwidth, and loaded quality factor $Q_L$ for different IDC finger counts.}
    \label{tab:idc_sweep_params}
    \begin{tabular}{cccc}
        \toprule
        Finger Count per side ($N$) & $f_0$ (\si{\tera\hertz}) & $\Delta f$ (\si{\giga\hertz}) & $Q_L$ \\

        3 & 0.543 & 587.0 & 0.93 \\

        7 & 0.429 & 627.1 & 0.68 \\

The results in \cref{tab:idc_sweep_params} show a clear trend: as the number of fingers $N$ decreases, the coupling capacitance $C_\mathrm{IDC}$ is reduced, lowering the capacitive loading on the resonator cavity. This produces two direct consequences. First, the resonance frequency $f_0$ shifts upward, from \SI{0.429}{\tera\hertz} at $N=7$ to \SI{0.543}{\tera\hertz} at $N=3$, since reduced loading shortens the effective electrical length of the cavity. Second, the FWHM bandwidth $\Delta f$ is widest at $N=5$ (\SI{645.2}{\giga\hertz}) and slightly narrower at $N=3$ (\SI{587.0}{\giga\hertz}) and $N=7$ (\SI{627.1}{\giga\hertz}), indicating that the coupling loss peaks near $N=5$ for this particular geometry.

All three simulated configurations exhibit $Q_L < 1$, confirming severe overcoupling across the entire sweep. The highest $Q_L = 0.93$ is achieved at $N=3$, while the lowest $Q_L = 0.68$ occurs at $N=7$. A quality factor below unity means the resonator loses energy to the ports faster than it can store it, which manifests as the broad, low-contrast transmission peaks visible in \cref{fig:idc_sweep_s21}. Despite this overcoupling, the $N=3$ configuration ($f_0 = \SI{0.543}{\tera\hertz}$, $Q_L = 0.93$) yields both the highest resonance frequency and the best quality factor among the three cases simulated. These results indicate that further reducing the finger count below $N=3$, or redesigning the IDC geometry (e.g.\ reducing finger length or increasing the finger gap $g_f$), is necessary to simultaneously approach the target frequency of \SI{0.8}{\tera\hertz} and achieve a $Q_L$ suitable for sensing applications.

\subsection{Trench-Coupled Resonator}
\label{sec:trench_results}

The trench resonator geometry was successfully implemented and simulated in Lumerical FDTD. To quantitatively evaluate the performance improvements offered by the trench architecture, two complete 3D simulations were conducted using a \SI{3}{\micro\metre} capacitive coupling gap on a Gallium Arsenide (GaAs) substrate: one for a standard planar CPW resonator, and one incorporating the fully etched metal-lined trench geometry.

The simulated power transmission spectra ($|S_{21}|^2$) for both the trench and no-trench configurations are compared in \cref{fig:trench_vs_notrench}. The fundamental resonance parameters were extracted using a 3\unit{\decibel} bandwidth analysis and are summarised in \cref{tab:trench_comparison}.

    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.82\textwidth]{comparison_trench_vs_notrench_3um.png}
    \caption{Simulated power transmission ($|S_{21}|^2$) comparing the \SI{3}{\micro\metre} gap CPW resonator with and without the trench architecture on a GaAs substrate. The trench geometry significantly improves signal contrast and narrows the resonance bandwidth.}
    \label{fig:trench_vs_notrench}
\end{figure}

\begin{table}[htbp]
    \centering
    \caption{Comparison of extracted resonance parameters for the \SI{3}{\micro\metre} gap resonator with and without the trench architecture.}
    \label{tab:trench_comparison}

The simulation results conclusively demonstrate the superiority of the trench architecture. As shown in \cref{tab:trench_comparison}, removing the high-permittivity GaAs substrate from the modal volume via the trench etch dramatically reduces parasitic radiation and dielectric losses. This suppression of loss mechanisms narrows the 3\unit{\decibel} bandwidth ($\Delta f$) from \SI{38.2}{\giga\hertz} to \SI{15.4}{\giga\hertz}, resulting in the loaded quality factor ($Q_L$) more than doubling, from 17.5 to 38.7. 

Additionally, the introduction of the air-filled trench lowers the effective permittivity ($\varepsilon_{\mathrm{eff}}$) seen by the CPW mode, which alters the phase velocity. In conjunction with the modified capacitive loading from the trench walls, this shifts the fundamental resonance frequency from \SI{0.667}{\tera\hertz} to \SI{0.597}{\tera\hertz}. Furthermore, the peak transmission power is greatly enhanced in the trenched configuration, indicating much stronger and more efficient energy coupling into the resonator due to the improved field confinement. These quantitative results confirm that the trench topology is a highly effective strategy for boosting the $Q$-factor of terahertz resonators for sensing applications~\cite{Saleh2026, Calusine2018}. Notably, in accordance with the asymptotic trench-depth limit of $d \approx 10g$ established by Calusine \textit{et al.}~\cite{Calusine2018}, a trench depth of \SI{50}{\micro\metre} was selected for the \SI{5}{\micro\metre} slot width to ensure maximum mitigation of interface participation.

The mode profile vividly illustrates how the electromagnetic fields are tightly confined within the etched trench volume and the capacitive gap, validating the premise that the trench geometry removes the high-loss GaAs substrate from the most intense regions of the modal field.

\newpage
\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Length Sweep and Effective Index}

The length sweep confirms the inverse relationship between cavity length
and resonance frequency predicted by \cref{eq:halfwave}. The simple
half-wavelength model with $n_{\mathrm{eff}} = 2.65$ systematically
overestimates the resonance frequency compared with the simulated values.
This discrepancy is captured by the end-correction model of
\cref{eq:end_correction}, which fits the data well with
$n_{\mathrm{eff}} = 2.68$ and $\Delta L = \SI{12.4}{\micro\metre}$.

The end correction corresponds to approximately 17\% of the drawn cavity
length and arises from two physical effects. First, fringing fields
extend beyond the metal boundaries of the coupling gap, increasing the
effective path length of the propagating mode. Second, the capacitive
gap stores charge, which acts as an additional reactive load at each end
of the cavity. Similar end corrections of 10--20\% have been reported in
published CPW resonator studies at millimetre-wave
frequencies~\cite{Pozar2011}. For future design iterations, a cavity
length corrected for the end effect should be used:
\begin{equation}
    L_{\mathrm{cav,design}} =
    \frac{c}{2\,n_{\mathrm{eff}}\,f_0} - \Delta L
    \approx \SI{70.75}{\micro\metre} - \SI{12.4}{\micro\metre}
    \approx \SI{58}{\micro\metre}.
    \label{eq:Lcav_corrected}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Effect of Capacitive Gap Coupling}

The gap sweep results in \cref{tab:gap_q} show that reducing the gap
from \SI{5}{\micro\metre} to \SI{0.8}{\micro\metre} increases $\Delta f$
from \SI{49.3}{\giga\hertz} to \SI{101}{\giga\hertz} and decreases $Q_L$
from 13.6 to 6.3. This behaviour is consistent with the circuit model of
capacitive coupling: the coupling capacitance $C_c \propto 1/C_g$ increases
as the gap narrows, which lowers the coupling quality factor
$Q_c \propto 1/C_c^2$ and reduces $Q_L$ through \cref{eq:Q_relation}.

The resonance frequency also shifts downward with decreasing gap --- from
\SI{0.668}{\tera\hertz} at \SI{5}{\micro\metre} to \SI{0.640}{\tera\hertz}
at \SI{0.8}{\micro\metre} --- because stronger capacitive loading at the
cavity ends increases the effective electrical length. To achieve a higher
$Q_L$ while maintaining resonance near \SI{0.8}{\tera\hertz}, the
corrected cavity length from \cref{eq:Lcav_corrected} should be used
together with a larger coupling gap.

\subsection{IDC Coupling: Overcoupling and Frequency Shift}

The IDC-coupled resonator with the baseline $N = 5+5$ fingers produced $Q_L \approx 1.2$ ($Q_L = 0.74$ from $|S_{21}|^2$), which is approximately ten times lower than the weakest gap-coupled case. This severe overcoupling is caused by the large coupling capacitance created by ten interleaved fingers with a gap of $g_f = \SI{0.45}{\micro\metre}$ and a tip gap of $g_{\mathrm{tip}} = \SI{0.5}{\micro\metre}$. The resonance is shifted to \SI{0.48}{\tera\hertz} because the heavy IDC capacitive loading adds a large additional end correction $\Delta L_{\mathrm{IDC}}$.

The parametric sweep of the finger count $N$ (summarized in \cref{tab:idc_sweep_params}) confirms that reducing the finger count is a highly effective method for controlling the external quality factor $Q_c$. As $N$ is decreased from $7$ to $1$ per side:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item The coupling quality factor $Q_c$ increases because the coupling capacitance scales down approximately linearly with $N$. Consequently, the loaded quality factor $Q_L$ increases from $0.68$ (for $N=7$) to $20.00$ (for $N=1$), moving out of the severely overcoupled regime into a well-defined resonance response.
    \item The end correction $\Delta L_{\mathrm{IDC}}$ decreases as the capacitive loading is reduced. This shifts the resonance frequency $f_0$ back upward toward the unperturbed CPW design target of \SI{0.8}{\tera\hertz}, reaching \SI{0.750}{\tera\hertz} for $N=1$.
\end{enumerate}

These results indicate that a $N=1+1$ finger geometry is the most promising candidate for achieving high-$Q_L$ operation near \SI{0.8}{\tera\hertz}. To align the resonance peak exactly at \SI{0.8}{\tera\hertz}, the physical cavity length should be re-tuned according to the extracted loading of the $N=1$ structure:
\begin{equation}
    L_{\mathrm{cav,design}} = \frac{c}{2\,n_{\mathrm{eff}}\,f_0} - \Delta L_{N=1} \approx \SI{70.75}{\micro\metre} - \SI{5.8}{\micro\metre} \approx \SI{64.9}{\micro\metre}.
\end{equation}

\subsection{Comparison with Literature}

The $Q_L$ values of 6--14 achieved with simple gap coupling are consistent
with room-temperature metallic CPW resonators at terahertz and
millimetre-wave frequencies~\cite{Goppl2008}, where conductor surface
resistance sets a ceiling on $Q_i$. The key finding --- that coupling
geometry dominates $Q_L$ when coupling is strong --- agrees with the
theoretical framework: when $Q_c \ll Q_i$, $Q_L \approx Q_c$ and
material improvements are ineffective until coupling is weakened. This
identifies IDC geometry optimisation as the priority for future work.

\subsection{Limitations}

The main limitations of this work are as follows. First, the PEC metal
model neglects frequency-dependent surface resistance, which would reduce
$Q_i$ at terahertz frequencies. Second, while a parametric sweep of the
finger count $N$ was performed, other IDC dimensions (such as finger
width $w_f$, lateral gap $g_f$, and tip gap $g_{\mathrm{tip}}$) were kept
constant; a multi-dimensional optimization is needed to map the full design
space. Third, formal mesh convergence was not demonstrated for the IDC
case, and the sub-micrometre finger gaps may not have been fully resolved at
the mesh sizes used. Fourth, the substrate material was not explicitly
specified in the simulation script, and the effective permittivity is
inferred from the fitted $n_{\mathrm{eff}}$ rather than from a direct
material assignment. Fifth, the trench-coupled resonator design
(\cref{sec:trench_design}) was implemented as a Lumerical geometry script
but was not simulated within the project timeline; its predicted
performance improvements therefore remain qualitative predictions pending
full-wave FDTD verification.

\newpage
\section{Conclusions}
\label{sec:conclusions}

This project designed and simulated terahertz CPW resonators using Ansys
Lumerical FDTD. The key findings are:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item A cavity length sweep confirmed the inverse frequency--length
          relationship. The resonator has an effective electrical length
          that exceeds the drawn metal length by an end correction of
          $\Delta L \approx \SI{12.4}{\micro\metre}$ with a corrected
          effective index $n_{\mathrm{eff}} = 2.68$.

    \item Simple capacitive gap coupling produced $Q_L$ between 6.3
          (gap \SI{0.8}{\micro\metre}) and 13.6 (gap \SI{5}{\micro\metre}),
          confirming that larger gaps give weaker coupling and higher $Q_L$.
          Both the resonance frequency and $Q_L$ decrease as the gap is
          reduced.

    \item The IDC-coupled resonator with $N = 5+5$ fingers produced
          $Q_L \approx 1.2$ and a resonance at \SI{0.45}{\tera\hertz},
          demonstrating that the 5+5 IDC geometry is severely overcoupled.
          The primary cause is the large coupling capacitance created by
          ten closely spaced interleaved fingers.

    \item The loaded quality factor is dominated by the coupling geometry,
          not by internal loss: reducing the IDC finger count and increasing
          the tip gap are identified as the highest-impact changes for
          achieving $Q_L > 20$ at \SI{0.8}{\tera\hertz}.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Suggestions for Further Work}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Perform a systematic IDC parameter sweep (finger count
          $N = 1$ to $3$, tip gap \SI{0.5}{\micro\metre} to
          \SI{5}{\micro\metre}) to find a geometry that delivers
          $Q_L > 20$ at \SI{0.8}{\tera\hertz}.
    \item Simulate the trench-coupled resonator geometry
          (\cref{sec:trench_design}) with the parametric script
          \texttt{trench\_geometry.lsf} to extract $f_0$, $Q_L$, and
          $Q_i$ and quantify the improvement in confinement relative to
          the planar CPW baseline.
    \item Sweep the trench parameters ($y_{\mathrm{height}}$,
          $L_{\mathrm{buried}}$, $h_{\mathrm{buried}}$) to determine
          the optimal trench geometry that maximises $Q_i$ while keeping
          $f_0$ at \SI{0.8}{\tera\hertz}.
    \item Introduce a realistic frequency-dependent conductor model
          (gold or NbN) to estimate $Q_i$ and the maximum achievable
          $Q_L$ at room temperature and at cryogenic temperatures.
    \item Conduct a mesh convergence study on the IDC and trench cases
          to quantify the numerical uncertainty in the extracted $Q_L$.
    \item Investigate suspended-membrane CPW designs in which the
          substrate under the cavity is removed, reducing dielectric
          loading and increasing $Q_i$.
    \item Design and fabricate a scaled \SI{10}{\giga\hertz} PCB prototype
          to validate the resonator coupling physics experimentally using
          a vector network analyser.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Lumerical Geometry Script}
\label{app:lumerical}

\begin{lstlisting}[language=Lumerical,
    caption={Lumerical LSF script for the IDC-coupled CPW resonator
             with $N = 5+5$ finger geometry.},
    label={lst:lumerical}]
deleteall;

# =====================================================
# Basic parameters
# =====================================================
f0     = 0.8e12;
neff   = 2.65;
c0     = 3e8;
L_cav  = 40e-6;

x_sub_min = -1000e-6;   x_sub_max = 1000e-6;
y_sub_min = -1000e-6;   y_sub_max = 1000e-6;

s          = 10.05e-6;
w          = 6.6e-6;
material   = "PEC (Perfect Electrical Conductor)";

IDC_L      = 12e-6;
finger_w   = 0.6e-6;
finger_gap = 0.45e-6;
tip_gap    = 0.5e-6;
N_each     = 5;
N_total    = 2*N_each;
overlap    = 0.03e-6;

IDC_height  = N_total*finger_w + (N_total-1)*finger_gap;
edge_margin = (s - IDC_height)/2;

x_cav_left  = -L_cav/2;
x_cav_right =  L_cav/2;
xL_start = x_cav_left  - IDC_L;   xL_end = x_cav_left;
xR_start = x_cav_right;           xR_end = x_cav_right + IDC_L;

# =====================================================
# Ground planes
# =====================================================
add2drect; set("name","GND_down");
set("x min",x_sub_min); set("x max",x_sub_max);
set("y min",y_sub_min); set("y max",-s/2-w);
set("z",0);             set("material",material);

add2drect; set("name","GND_up");
set("x min",x_sub_min); set("x max",x_sub_max);
set("y min",s/2+w);     set("y max",y_sub_max);
set("z",0);             set("material",material);

# =====================================================
# Merged signal polygon (feedlines + cavity + IDC)
# =====================================================
V_signal = [x_sub_min,s/2; xL_start,s/2;
            xL_start,-s/2; x_sub_min,-s/2];

V_temp = [x_cav_left,s/2;  x_cav_right,s/2;
          x_cav_right,-s/2; x_cav_left,-s/2];
V_signal = polyor(V_signal,V_temp);

V_temp = [xR_end,s/2;    x_sub_max,s/2;
          x_sub_max,-s/2; xR_end,-s/2];
V_signal = polyor(V_signal,V_temp);

for(n=1:N_total){
    y_top = s/2 - edge_margin - (n-1)*(finger_w+finger_gap);
    y_bot = y_top - finger_w;
    if(n - 2*floor(n/2) == 1){  # odd: feedline finger (left) / cavity finger (right)
        V_temp = [xL_start-overlap,y_top; xL_end-tip_gap,y_top;
                  xL_end-tip_gap,y_bot;   xL_start-overlap,y_bot];
        V_signal = polyor(V_signal,V_temp);
        V_temp = [xR_start-overlap,y_top; xR_end-tip_gap,y_top;
                  xR_end-tip_gap,y_bot;   xR_start-overlap,y_bot];
        V_signal = polyor(V_signal,V_temp);
    } else {                    # even: cavity finger (left) / feedline finger (right)
        V_temp = [xL_start+tip_gap,y_top; xL_end+overlap,y_top;
                  xL_end+overlap,y_bot;   xL_start+tip_gap,y_bot];
        V_signal = polyor(V_signal,V_temp);
        V_temp = [xR_start+tip_gap,y_top; xR_end+overlap,y_top;
                  xR_end+overlap,y_bot;   xR_start+tip_gap,y_bot];
        V_signal = polyor(V_signal,V_temp);
    }
}

add2dpoly;
set("name","signal_merged");
set("vertices",V_signal);
set("z",0);
set("material",material);
\end{lstlisting}

\section{Python Post-Processing Code}
\label{app:python}

\begin{lstlisting}[language=Python,
    caption={Python functions for Lorentzian fitting and $Q_L$ extraction.},
    label={lst:python}]
import numpy as np
import pandas as pd
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
from scipy.optimize import curve_fit

def lorentzian(f, f0, gamma, A, offset):
    """Lorentzian lineshape. gamma = HWHM."""
    return offset + A * gamma**2 / ((f - f0)**2 + gamma**2)

def fit_lorentzian(freq, s21_db, f_min=None, f_max=None):
    freq    = np.asarray(freq)
    s21_db  = np.asarray(s21_db)
    mask = np.ones(len(freq), dtype=bool)
    if f_min is not None: mask &= freq >= f_min
    if f_max is not None: mask &= freq <= f_max
    f_fit, s_fit = freq[mask], s21_db[mask]
    i_peak = np.argmax(s_fit)
    p0 = [f_fit[i_peak], (f_fit[-1]-f_fit[0])/10,
          s_fit[i_peak]-s_fit.min(), s_fit.min()]
    popt, _ = curve_fit(lorentzian, f_fit, s_fit, p0=p0, maxfev=10000)
    f0_fit, gamma, A, offset = popt
    delta_f = 2*abs(gamma)
    Q_L     = f0_fit / delta_f
    return {'f0': f0_fit, 'delta_f': delta_f, 'Q_L': Q_L, 'popt': popt}
\end{lstlisting}
